Heat Pump Installation in Toronto & the GTA

Cold-climate heat pumps have changed the math for Toronto homes. Modern inverter systems hold useful heating capacity down to −25°C, deliver three or more units of heat for every unit of electricity, and cool the house in summer from the same equipment. For many GTA homeowners that means one system replaces both the furnace and the air conditioner — with meaningful rebates available.

Leadder designs each installation around your actual house. We run a heat-loss calculation, review your ductwork and electrical service, and determine your balance point — the outdoor temperature below which backup heat is needed. That number drives the entire design and it is what separates a heat pump that performs from one that leaves a family cold in a deep freeze.

From there we lay out the honest options. A full electric changeover with sized backup suits well-insulated homes. A dual-fuel setup pairs the heat pump with your existing or a new gas furnace, letting the controls switch automatically to whichever source is cheaper at any given temperature — usually the best fit for older Toronto housing stock. Ductless multi-zone works where there are no ducts at all.

We handle rebate paperwork, permits, electrical coordination, refrigerant line work, full commissioning, and a walkthrough of how the controls actually behave in winter so nothing about the system surprises you in January.

Designing a heat pump that works in a Toronto winter

Balance point is the whole design

The balance point is the outdoor temperature below which the heat pump alone can no longer keep up and backup heat is needed. Set it correctly and you run cheap heat almost all winter. Get it wrong and you run expensive backup for months. We calculate it from your home's actual heat loss rather than picking equipment off a chart.

All-electric, dual-fuel, or ductless

A full electric changeover with properly sized backup suits well-insulated homes. Dual-fuel pairs the heat pump with a gas furnace and switches automatically to whichever source is cheaper at a given temperature — usually the best fit for older Toronto housing stock. Ductless multi-zone works where there are no ducts to use.

Efficiency ratings and what they mean here

HSPF2 rates heating efficiency and SEER2 rates cooling. What matters more in our climate is rated capacity at low ambient temperature — how much heat the unit still makes at −15°C or −25°C. A high seasonal rating with poor cold-weather capacity is the wrong machine for Toronto.

Ductwork, electrical and rebates

Heat pumps move more air at a lower temperature than a furnace, so returns and duct sizing often need attention. Electrical service and breaker capacity get checked before we quote, not after. We handle the rebate paperwork, the permit, and a walkthrough of how the controls behave in winter so nothing surprises you in January.

Heat Pump Installation FAQs

What are the signs I need heat pump installation?

A heat pump is worth considering if:

  • You want heating and cooling from one system
  • You're looking to lower your carbon footprint
  • You want to take advantage of available rebates
  • Your AC is due for replacement anyway
  • You're building or renovating
  • You want efficient comfort with lower operating costs
What does the Leadder heat pump installation process look like?
  1. Home comfort assessment: We evaluate your home, existing equipment, ductwork, and comfort concerns.
  2. Load & sizing calculation: A proper Manual-J style load calc sizes the system to your home — not a rule of thumb.
  3. Equipment recommendation: We recommend options at different efficiency and budget levels and explain the trade-offs.
  4. Written estimate: A clear, itemized quote — including permits and warranty registration — before you commit.
  5. Professional installation: Clean, code-compliant installation with permits pulled and handled for you.
  6. Testing & walkthrough: We commission the system, verify performance, and walk you through operation.
Heat pump options

We match the right system and configuration to your home:

  • Cold-climate ducted — Whole-home heating and cooling through your existing ductwork.
  • Ductless heat pumps — Zone-by-zone comfort for homes without ducts — see ductless mini-splits.
  • Hybrid (dual-fuel) — Heat pump paired with a furnace for efficiency across all temperatures.
  • Air-to-water options — Ask us about options for hydronic and radiant homes.
Heat pumps for GTA homes

Cold-climate heat pumps are a strong fit for Toronto's climate, especially as a dual-fuel hybrid that leans on the heat pump for most of the season and a furnace for the coldest days. We design systems that make sense for how GTA homes are built and heated.

Do heat pumps work in Toronto winters?

Yes. Cold-climate heat pumps are designed to heat efficiently well below freezing. Many GTA homeowners pair one with a furnace for the coldest stretches.

Will a heat pump lower my bills?

It can, especially versus electric or oil heat, and it replaces your AC too. Savings depend on your current system and rates.

What rebates are available?

Heat pumps often qualify for manufacturer and government rebates. We help identify and document what applies to you.

Ducted or ductless?

If you have ductwork, a ducted heat pump serves the whole home. If not, ductless is an excellent option.

Do I still need a furnace?

Not always, but a hybrid setup with a furnace is popular in our climate for peak-cold reliability.
